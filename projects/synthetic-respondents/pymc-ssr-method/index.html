LLMs as Synthetic Consumers

How Semantic Similarity Rating finally makes AI survey responses realistic

The Stakes

Consumer research costs billions — and it's still unreliable

The scale

Companies spend billions of dollars annually on consumer panel surveys before launching products

The problem

Panels suffer from acquiescence bias, positivity bias, satisficing — and they're slow and expensive

The central question of most surveys: Purchase Intent (PI) — rated on a 1–5 Likert scale. Getting this right predicts product success.

So researchers wondered: could LLMs simulate consumers and replace or augment expensive panels?

The Idea

Synthetic consumers: LLMs impersonating real people

Give an LLM a demographic persona — age, gender, income, region — then show it a product concept. Ask it to rate purchase intent.

Prior work showed this is directionally promising — LLMs do track product appeal — but the ratings themselves look nothing like human distributions.

The culprit: everyone was asking LLMs to pick a number. That's the wrong question.

The Problem with Direct Ratings

Ask for a number, get a boring number

When LLMs are constrained to reply with "1, 2, 3, 4, or 5", they overwhelmingly pick 3. They regress to the center.

Human surveys: most responses cluster at 4 and 5. LLMs via DLR: almost entirely 3.

The problem is in how you ask, not in the model's knowledge. LLMs understand product appeal — they just can't express it naturally on a 1–5 numeric scale.

The Solution

Semantic Similarity Rating (SSR)

Stop asking for numbers. Ask for words — then map words to numbers using embeddings.

SSR Core Insight

Let the LLM speak freely, then measure where it lands

Step 1

The AI generates a free-text response about purchase intent — no number constraint

Step 2

The response is converted into a numerical "meaning fingerprint" that captures the sentiment

(This is called embedding — a standard text-to-numbers conversion)

Step 3

Compare the fingerprint against 5 anchor statements, one per rating point

(Similarity = how close in meaning — 1.0 = identical, 0 = unrelated)

"I'm somewhat interested. If it works well and isn't too expensive, I might give it a try."

Example AI response — maps naturally to a 3–4 distribution

SSR Core Insight

From words to a probability distribution

Instead of forcing a single number, SSR produces the full spread of probabilities across all 5 rating points — closer to how real human responses are distributed.

This means you can see: 5% chance of rating 1, 10% chance of rating 2, 35% chance of rating 3, 40% chance of rating 4, 10% chance of rating 5. That's a much richer signal than just "3.4 out of 5."

SSR Pipeline

From free text to a Likert distribution

The synthetic consumer speaks. The embedding model listens. The anchor set translates.

Anchor statements (5 total, one per Likert point):

1 → "It's rather unlikely I'd buy it."

3 → "I might consider buying it."

5 → "It's very likely I'd buy it."

Cosine similarity between the response embedding and each anchor embedding → proportional to Likert probability

They use OpenAI's text-embedding-3-small and average over 6 different reference statement sets to reduce noise.

How It Works

Meaning measured by direction

Each piece of text — the free-text response and each anchor phrase — gets converted into a list of numbers that captures its meaning. Think of it as a GPS coordinate in "idea space."

Two GPS points close together = similar meaning. Points far apart = different meaning.

This is called cosine similarity — a standard way of comparing text meanings without needing any training data.

The response embedding is compared to all 5 anchor embeddings. The closer the meaning, the more probability weight that anchor gets. The result is normalized to sum to 100%.

No AI training needed. No labeled survey data. One set of 5 anchor phrases, crafted once, works across all product categories.

Experimental Setup

Three methods head-to-head

Tested on 57 consumer surveys for personal care products from a leading corporation. 9,300 unique human participants total.

DLR

Direct Likert Rating — just ask for 1–5

FLR

Follow-up Likert Rating — text first, then same LLM maps to number

SSR

Semantic Similarity Rating — text first, then embedding cosine similarity

Both GPT-4o and Gemini-2-flash were tested. Two metrics: distributional similarity (K^xy) and correlation attainment (ρ).

The distributional similarity gap is the headline story: Direct Likert Rating gets 0.26, Semantic Similarity Rating gets 0.88 — a 3× improvement in capturing how opinions actually spread across the scale.

Distribution Quality

SSR distributions look human

The key advance: SSR produces realistic spread across the 1–5 scale, not the "3-spike" seen with direct rating.

For DLR: nearly all responses pile on 3. KS similarity ≈ 0.26 — barely above random.

For FLR: better spread, but still too narrow around the mean. KS similarity ≈ 0.72.

For SSR: the distribution closely mirrors human data — proper spread, correct shape. KS similarity ≈ 0.88.

This matters because a product getting 40% "5s" and 30% "4s" is very different from one getting 60% "3s" — even if the mean is similar.

Demographic Conditioning

Demographics matter — but not all equally

Works well

Age — younger and older consumers rate lower than middle-aged. The AI replicates this pattern correctly.

Works well

Income — the AI captures budget sensitivity well, especially when the persona statement references price constraints.

Use with caution

Gender — the AI sometimes reverses direction vs. human data. Treat gender splits as directional only.

Use with caution

Region — geographic purchasing patterns are not well captured. Avoid regional sub-group analysis.

Demographic Conditioning

Without demographics, the signal collapses

When no demographic persona is given, the AI rates nearly every product 4 or 5 out of 5. All products look equal. Correlation attainment drops from 90% to 50% — barely better than random.

Practical implication: Always include demographic personas — age, income, and relevant lifestyle descriptors. This is what makes SSR useful for product differentiation rather than just general sentiment.

Unexpected Bonus

Free text = free qualitative research

Because SSR requires free-text responses first, you get rich qualitative feedback as a byproduct — something direct Likert ratings can't provide.

"The ease of use and the promise of no sensitivity are appealing. Plus, it's from a trusted brand."

Synthetic consumer on a high-rated concept

"It seems a bit too high-end for my needs and budget. These body parts don't usually bother me."

Synthetic consumer on a low-rated concept

Unlike human open-ends ("It's good", "Not much"), LLM responses are information-rich, highlight features, surface objections, and explain the rating — all automatically.

Comparison

SSR beats a supervised LightGBM model

They trained 300 machine learning models (LightGBM classifiers) on historical demographic and product data — the type of proprietary model a large company might actually build — testing on held-out surveys.

65%

LightGBM correlation attainment

88–90%

The AI approach — which needs no training data or setup at all (called "zero-shot") — outperforms a custom model trained on years of actual survey data. The reason: AI understands product descriptions semantically, not just as data rows.

Limitations

Where Semantic Similarity Rating still falls short

Anchor wording sensitivity

Slightly different anchor phrases produce slightly different distributions. The paper averages across 6 phrase variants to reduce this noise.

Domain dependence

Works well for personal care and consumer goods — categories the AI has seen extensively. Novel or niche domains may produce unreliable results.

Gender and region

Sub-group analyses by gender and region are unreliable — the AI sometimes reverses the direction. Use these cuts as directional signals only.

Real purchasing behavior

Cannot account for price, shelf availability, promotions, or competitive context. Measures attitude, not behavior.

Limitations

Best fit vs. poor fit

Good fit

  • Early-stage concept screening
  • High-volume testing (50+ concepts)
  • Consumer goods, personal care, food
  • Age and income segmentation

Poor fit

  • Final go/no-go decisions alone
  • Gender or regional deep-dives
  • Niche or technical products
  • Predicting actual purchase rates

Use SSR to narrow the field. Use human panels to make the final call. The two methods are complements, not substitutes.

Part 3

What This Changes

The practical implications for consumer and market research

Practical Implications

A new screening layer before human panels

SSR enables a new workflow: run synthetic panels first to screen concepts, reserve expensive human panels for top candidates.

Before SSR

Every concept idea → expensive human survey → weeks of wait → decision

After SSR

50 concepts → cheap synthetic screen → top 5 → human survey → decision

No setup required. Works across product categories from day one — no labeled data, no custom model to build or maintain.

Bonus: the free-text responses explain why concepts score the way they do — automatic qualitative feedback no numeric rating can provide.
