Paper #2 · Argyle et al. · 2022

Out of One, Many

Using Language Models to Simulate Human Samples

A single LLM contains the statistical patterns of every human sub-population it was trained on.
This paper shows you how to pull them out — one person at a time.

Brigham Young University · Political Science + Computer Science

The Problem

Running Surveys Is Hard

Representative surveys take weeks and cost tens of thousands of dollars.

Limited to wealthy countries, English speakers, easy-to-reach populations.

Huge swaths of human opinion: systematically under-surveyed.

What if you could run a survey on a million synthetic people — for $29?

The Insight

GPT-3 Is Not One Thing

Most people think an LLM has a single "bias" — it's liberal, or Western, or tech-skewed.

The authors argue the opposite: it contains many distributions simultaneously — one per human sub-population.

The right conditioning text navigates you to the right sub-population's region of the model.

First Principles

What GPT-3 Actually Is

A language model is a conditional probability distribution over tokens.

P(xn | x1, x2, ..., xn-1)

Probability of next token, given all previous tokens

Before it can generate, it needs conditioning text — the context that shifts the whole output distribution.

Context = "Can you come" → high P for "home"
Context = "Can you eat" → high P for "this"

The Key Concept

Algorithmic Fidelity

How well does a model's internal patterns of relationships between ideas and attitudes match those within real human sub-populations?

"The model doesn't just contain one bias. It contains many. And by conditioning properly, you can select from them."

Argyle et al., 2022

Algorithmic Fidelity

Four Criteria to Prove It

Criterion 1

Social Science Turing Test

Human evaluators can't reliably distinguish GPT-3 responses from real human responses.

Criterion 2

Backward Continuity

Reading the response, you could infer who wrote it — their demographics, politics, identity.

Criterion 3

Forward Continuity

The response flows naturally from the conditioning context — it doesn't contradict the backstory.

Criterion 4 ★

Pattern Correspondence

The correlations between variables (ideology ↔ vote, education ↔ party) match real-world data.

The Method

Silicon Sampling

Generate a virtual population — each person is a GPT-3 completion conditioned on a demographic backstory.

Demographics from ANES (American National Election Studies). GPT-3 supplies the responses.

Step by Step

Building a Silicon Individual

1. Take a real survey participant's demographics from ANES.

2. Fill a first-person template with their age, race, gender, income, party ID, ideology.

3. Append the survey question in first-person format.

4. Sample GPT-3's completion. That's your silicon response.

Repeat for every participant → you have a silicon sample.

The Core Math Problem

GPT-3's World Is Skewed

Define: V = vote choice. B = demographic background.

GPT-3 models both jointly:

P(V, BGPT3) = P(V|BGPT3) × P(BGPT3)

Problem: trained on internet text. Internet users ≠ Americans.

Younger, more male, more tech-savvy, more Western.

P(BGPT3) ≠ P(BTrue)

Ask GPT-3 "how does America vote?" — you get the internet's answer.

The Math Fix

Correcting Skewed Marginals

What we want:

P(V) = ∫ P(V|B) × P(BTrue) dB

Can't trust

P(BGPT3)

Who GPT-3 thinks exists — internet-skewed

Can trust

P(V|B)

How a given person responds — GPT-3 learned this well

Sample backstories from ANES. Compute:

P(V | BANES) × P(BANES)

Like Simpson's Paradox — aggregate GPT-3 misleads. Condition on real demographics: it's accurate.

Technical Detail

Turning Text Into Probabilities

For vote prediction, you need a probability, not just text.

Prompt: "In 2016, I voted for ___"

GPT-3 assigns log-probabilities to every possible next token. Problem: "Trump" is spread across many tokens: Donald, donald, Trump, trump, TRUMP...

Fix: Collapse all variants of each candidate → sum their probabilities → normalize so all candidates sum to 1.

P(Trump) = softmax( Σ log-P(token ∈ Trump-set) )

Evidence

Three Studies

Study 1

Social Science Turing Test

Can you tell GPT-3 text from human text?

Study 2

Vote Prediction

Does GPT-3 predict voting like humans across 3 elections?

Study 3

Complex Correlations

Do GPT-3's variable relationships match ANES correlation structure?

Study 1

Can You Tell Them Apart?

2,107 real people wrote 4-word lists describing Republicans and Democrats (Pigeonholing Partisans dataset).

GPT-3 silicon individuals — matched demographics — did the same.

2,873 independent evaluators rated 7,675 texts — blind to source.

Judged: tone, extremity, traits, issues, groups. Guessed: human or computer?

What they found:

Evaluators correctly labeled 61.7% of human texts as human — and 61.2% of GPT-3 texts as human. Difference: p = 0.44 (not significant).

Study 1 · Deeper Result

Same Patterns, Right Exceptions

On all 5 characteristics (tone, extremity, traits, issues, groups) — GPT-3 and human lists scored nearly identically.

The only outlier: "traits" was mentioned far more than anything else — in both human AND GPT-3 data. GPT-3 reproduced this quirk without being told to.

GPT-3 mirrored not just the averages — but the anomaly. That's deep pattern correspondence.

Partisanship was also detectable: readers could guess the political identity of list writers at above-chance rates for both human and silicon lists. The political signal embedded by conditioning is real.

Study 2

Predicting Presidential Votes

ANES data from 2012, 2016, and 2020 elections. 10 demographic variables embedded per backstory.

GPT-3 asked to complete: "In [year], I voted for ___"

Binary vote extracted via log-probability. Compared to actual ANES vote.

2020 is outside GPT-3's training window.

Training ended 2019 — tests generalizability.

Study 2 · Key Detail

GPT-3 Failed in the Right Place

One exception: pure Independents — correlations as low as 0.02.

Political science has known this for decades: Independents are the hardest to predict. Most conflicted, least engaged, least likely to vote.

GPT-3 failed exactly where real data fails. It's modeling genuine uncertainty.

Over half of all subgroup correlations: 0.90 or higher.

Study 3 · The Hardest Test

Does the Whole Web of Correlations Match?

Not just toplines — the full correlation structure.

11 variables: gender, race, age, education, church, ideology, party ID, vote...

For every pair: compute Cramer's V in human data and GPT-3 data. Compare.

Cramer's V measures association between two categorical variables. 0 = no relationship, 1 = perfect.

V = √( χ² / (n × min(r−1, c−1)) )

Study 3 · Result

GPT-3 Reproduced the Whole Map

Where human correlations are strong (ideology ↔ party ID), GPT-3 correlations are also strong.

Where human correlations are weak (gender ↔ church attendance), GPT-3 also shows weak association.

The model doesn't invent relationships that don't exist. It doesn't miss ones that do. Mean error across all variable pairs: −0.026.

This is Criterion 4 — Pattern Correspondence — the hardest bar. GPT-3 cleared it across an 11-variable correlation matrix.

Important Limits

What This Paper Does NOT Claim

Not claimed

  • Simulate a specific individual accurately
  • Replace human data for causal inference
  • Generalizes to all domains automatically
  • Every response will be coherent

What IS claimed

  • Population-level distributions faithfully reproduced
  • Good for hypothesis generation and piloting
  • High fidelity in US political opinion
  • Cheap — Study 1 cost $29

Risk: this fidelity could enable targeted misinformation at scale. The authors flag it explicitly.

Summary

Out of One, Many

The bet: GPT-3 contains many human sub-populations. Conditioning on demographics navigates to the right one.

The fix: Use ANES for P(B) — who Americans are. Trust GPT-3 only for P(V|B) — how a given person responds. This corrects the internet-skewed training distribution.

The evidence: Tetrachoric r = 0.90–0.94 across three elections. Cramer's V mean error = −0.026. Turing test: p = 0.44. All criteria met.

The term: Algorithmic fidelity — 4 criteria: Turing test, backward continuity, forward continuity, pattern correspondence.
