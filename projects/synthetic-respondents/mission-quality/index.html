Mission Possible

Can you still collect high-quality data from online surveys in the age of AI agents?

The Stakes

Online surveys power social science — but the signal is getting noisier

The upside

Online surveys offer large, diverse, cost-effective samples — critical for social science at scale

The new threat

AI agents can now autonomously complete surveys. Participants may use LLMs to answer. Fraudulent accounts multiply responses.

The central question: Is any given survey response from an attentive human — or a bot, AI agent, or distracted respondent gaming the system?

Researchers need empirical evidence — not gut feeling — to know whether online data is trustworthy, and what to do about it.

The Study

One survey instrument, six respondent sources

The researchers ran the same survey across radically different populations — to build an empirical map of data quality.

Lab (Gold Standard) — 314 UC San Diego students, in-person, no LLM use allowed

AI Agents (Adversarial Baseline) — 240 submissions from ChatGPT Agent, Perplexity Comet, Claude in Chrome, and BrowserUse

4 Online Platforms — ~3,000 U.S. participants across Prolific, MTurk, Bilendi, and Moblab

The survey included a dictator game (split $10), an open-ended question explaining the decision, attention checks, and demographic questions.

The Method

5 Data Quality Checks

Simple, reusable, designed to catch AI agents, inattentive humans, and fraudulent accounts — without over-excluding real respondents.

Data Quality Checks

Five checks, each targeting a different failure mode

No single check catches everything. The combination is what works.

1. Classic Attention Check — "Select the option furthest to the left/right" on a Likert scale. Catches inattentive humans — but AI agents pass this 99% of the time.

2. Video Attention Check — Watch a short video, type the 4 numbers shown sequentially. Only 17% of AI agents pass. 97%+ of humans pass.

3. Typed Text — Did the respondent actually type the open-text response? Detects copy-paste, LLM injection, and automated text insertion. Only 45% of AI agents pass.

4. Typed with Typical Speed — Median keystroke interval >75ms. Flags ultra-fast (automated) text entry. Only 8% of AI agents pass vs 97% of lab participants.

5. Unique IP Address — Each response must come from a unique IP. Catches account fraud and duplicate submissions.

Baseline Results

Platform quality varies dramatically

The same survey, the same checks — but the share of respondents passing all five checks differs by a factor of 10× across platforms.

Prolific: 90% pass all checks in the first stage — close to the lab benchmark of 80%.

Bilendi: 73% · Moblab: 55% — moderate quality, large room for improvement with screening.

MTurk: only 9% pass all checks. The platform has severely degraded. Only 79 of 881 respondents qualify as high-quality.

AI agent pass rate: 0%. The five checks successfully eliminate all 240 AI agent submissions.

Key Insight

Different checks catch different problems — no single check is enough

Catches AI Agents

Video check (only 17% pass) and typing speed (only 8% pass) are the primary AI detectors

Catches Inattentive Humans

Classic attention checks catch humans who race through surveys — but AI agents pass these 99% of the time

Catches Account Fraud

Unique IP check — 21% of MTurk responses share duplicate IPs, consistent with account farming

Behavioral data alone cannot detect AI agents. AI agents make similar dictator game decisions (60% give $5, vs 71% of lab participants), and their demographics look human too.

The typing and video checks exploit a current technical limitation of AI agents — they process visual/keystroke information differently than humans. These checks may need to evolve as AI improves.

The Solution

The Two-Stage Recruitment Method

Screen first. Research second. Use quality checks to build a pool of verified respondents before running your actual study.

Two-Stage Recruitment

Run a short baseline survey first — invite only quality respondents for the real study

The approach mirrors field survey methodology: a screener stage before the main instrument.

Stage 1 — Baseline Survey: Short survey with the 5 quality checks. Broadly recruit from the platform. Identify who passes all checks.

Stage 2 — Main Study: Only re-invite respondents who passed all Stage 1 checks. Run your actual research instrument on this pre-verified pool.

This ex-ante screening also limits p-hacking opportunities, since the quality pool is defined before the main data collection begins.

The checks in Stage 1 are broad and reusable across most studies with minimal modification — the open-text question can be added to almost any survey.

Two-Stage Results

Second-stage quality converges to lab-level across platforms

After two-stage recruitment, the share of respondents passing all checks rises sharply on every viable platform.

Moblab: 55% → 95% — largest improvement, from moderate to excellent

Bilendi: 73% → 93% — substantial improvement

Prolific: 90% → 93% — already high quality; two-stage confirms and validates it

MTurk: too degraded to run Stage 2. Only 79 of 881 participants qualified — insufficient for a main study. The two-stage method revealed this early, saving wasted resources.

Takeaways

High-quality online data is still achievable — if you screen for it

The good news

Combining 5 simple quality checks with a two-stage recruitment method produces lab-comparable data quality on Prolific, Bilendi, and Moblab

The warning

Platform choice matters enormously. MTurk is severely degraded — only 9% pass quality checks. Two-stage method can't fix insufficient supply of quality respondents

The arms race

AI agents will improve. Today's video + typing checks exploit current AI limitations. Checks must evolve. The two-stage framework can accommodate new checks as they emerge

The practical prescription: run a short baseline survey with these 5 checks first. If enough respondents pass, run your main study on that pool. If not (like MTurk), you've saved time and money before wasting it on bad data.
